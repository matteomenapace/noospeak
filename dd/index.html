This diary documents the development of the project now labeled "NooSpeak", from the early stages of its concept till the latest implementation.
You will find material that can be considered illegal (for example, visual manipulations of trademarks and logos) as well as information about producing such illegal content. Furthermore, the content of this documentation and the content of NooSpeak have not been authored by Google, nor they represent the views or opinions of Google or Google personnel. NooSpeak is not affiliated or sponsored by Google, it only uses the publicly available service known as Google Search.
What is NooSpeak?
NooSpeak is a natural language search engine: it allows people to express their needs in a language they are familiar with, and it presents search results in the form of a dialogue (or chat, if you prefer). It uses weak artificial intelligence and clustered human information to help people refine their searches. It also aims to make web search an entertaining discovery process.

25 July 2006
Once upon a time..
It all started from "I barbari", by Alessandro Baricco. This book was published, split in chapters, in 2006 within the italian newspaper La Repubblica.
I stumbled upon one them when I came back from holidays in July 2006. It was about Google, obviously. If you understand Italian, read the full chapter here.
One fact caught my attention: according to the author, back in 1996 Inktomi (a very popular search engine at the time) could not find itself. Typing "inktomi" would not produce any result. "But can Google find itself?" I asked myself. Of course typing "google" into Google Search box returns thousands of results, but my question was different: considering self-awareness as the borderline between artificial intelligence and "real life", the question becomes "Is Google self aware? Is Google intelligent?". The answer is "No", which leads to the next question: "What if Google were really intelligent, i.e. self aware?"

12 October 2006
Keywords, keywords, keywords..
More notes witnessing my (humble) understanding/interpretation of several concepts before I started researching them:
Relational aesthetics: the attention is given to communication rather than to representation. A leading role is taken by the form (medium), rather than by the content. It could be human-human or human-machine, where aesthetics are the rules/conventions/practices used to shape the medium, regardless of the meaning conveyed by the content/message.
The medium is the message: each medium is an active metaphor that influences the communication of a message. Medium does not only apply to the expression plan, but also to the content plan of a "text". A communication mediated by the Internet is therefore influenced by remoteness, anonymity, lack of body language. Written text is (still) the main form, the Internet is the medium.
Corporate identity: it is made by the values (ethics, mission) of a corporation. All visual extensions of a corporation are declined following such values. Ideally, every aspect of a corporate communication and every product/service it provides should embody the values of its identity. Visually, all artifacts should be consistent and identifiable with it. In short, a CI is the idea/blueprint of a corporation.
Self-referentialism: it happens whenever a person or a communicating system (anything could be a system, but here I mean something that is deliberately expressing a message, rather than just witnessing or suggesting it) refers to herself: speaks about herself as a whole or about a part of herself, or uses herself as a reference mark.
Semantic Web: a phase of the WWW, or perhaps a series of programming practices and production conventions that aim to develop a standardized language understandable by computers. Data is intelligible by machines as the relations between data are made meaningful. The Web can be considered a huge database, an enormous quickly-expanding unordered collection of documents. Semantic Web means to create meaningful and standard relations between the data contained into the documents, to have data describing other data or data describing the relations between different bodies of data, in order for computer to infer logical properties about information (for better search results, automated tasks, etc.).
Natural language software: human languages are very complex and inconspicuous set of rules that determine our interpersonal communication. Software instead are coded in very strict languages that do not allow for incongruence. Natural language software converts information from computer databases into normal-sounding human language, and samples of human language into more formal representations that are easier for computer programs to manipulate.
Predicate logic: it is a branch of logic based on natural language, and its main components are subject, verb and object. Subject and object are entities, carriers of properties, while verb establishes relations between them and allows for logical inferences to be made on the two entities.
Collaborative work: a work which is not the effort of a single person, but the result of several people collaboration. This implies a certain level of interaction between people involved, so that the process of generating the work is as valuable as the end result. Collaboration requires compromise and negotiation but it can lead to unexpectedly creative and innovative results.
Economy of attention: related to the Web, where traffic and hype are the parameters for judging the value/price of an information. The more a website is visited, the more is valuable: therefore, a lot of efforts are put into driving users' attention, with search engines in a leading position.
Reverse engineering metaphors: metaphors are cognitive tools to make sense of reality. We use metaphors to assign functions, meaning and properties to entities that otherwise result difficult to understand. Reverse engineering means, to me, to challenge the familiarity of such metaphors, applying them to unfamiliar objects/events (meta-metaphors?) or bringing them back to their original meaning.

30 October 2006
Interface
The interface design started from analyzing three popular instant messaging clients: Windows Live Messenger, Google Talk and Skype. See comparative screenshots.

A chatting interface is a single window tiled in two main panes:
1) input box, positioned at the bottom, contains:

1.1) a blank field where user types her message (the necessary),

1.2) a system status box (delivering information about the activity on the other end of the communication, e.g: "Matteo is writing..")

1.3) sometimes a "submit" button (which can be avoided by using the "return/enter" key)

1.4) decorative functionalities, usually placed over the input field (font customization, images list, etc).
2) conversation log, positioned at the top and filling the space down to the bottom with a chronological list of entries, which are added at the bottom of the conversation list. Each entry displays:

2.1) author's name (nickname). In case of multiple entries some clients place "..." between each entry,

2.2) a typed message (text-based, but it can contain tiny emotional images, so-called emoticons, or animations)

2.3) additional information regarding the time when an entry was created.

The focal point of user interaction is the bottom of the interface window, where the input box is placed and the most recent entries are displayed. This is the opposite of a search engine interface, where the input box and the most relevant results are placed at the top of the page, leaving room for scrolling the rest of the list with content navigation at the bottom.
My first designs applied the bottom focal point to a Google's typical results page.

04 November 2006
The entry point
The start page of the application should clearly present one entry point, make it task-oriented and enrich it with few options.
Google's home page is simply self-explanatory, as Jenifer Tidwell states in "Designing Interfaces": "its home page design focuses users' attention on that one thing: you can't miss the search box! The other stuff is ranked into secondary and tertiary tiers, plus utility navigation".
I thought my homepage would resemble quite exactly that of Google, to introduce users with a familiar context and encourage them to perform a familiar action, yet delivering a surprising experience once they start typing anything in the search box: the apparent search box was transformed into a yes/no gateway to the main application.

06 November 2006
User testing pt.1
Testing the unexpected Google homepage approach gave interesting results: anyone recognized a familiar website, but few would actually notice the small differences. Moreover, discovering that it was actually impossible to type anything in the search box lead most users to a sense of frustration (rather than curiosity, as I expected) that discouraged them from continuing the experience. In fact, just one out of nine "typed" the whole Google's invitation to chat and clicked "Yes", while all others stopped after a few keystrokes and started clicking around.
A softer, more open approach was needed, as well as a visual layout that would clearly draw the difference between Google Search and my application.

11 November 2006
InterFace
Being my aim to exploit functional and familiar interface elements to produce an unconventional/unexpected user experience, I began to be interested in Form Art, a branch of net.art started by Alexei Shulgin and others in 1997. I started thinking the interface as an inter-face, where formal elements represented human features: an input box becomes a mouth, a radio button becomes an eye, and so on.
My inspiration for Google's face was the Mouth of Truth sculpture in Rome: it is thought to be part of an ancient Roman fountain, or perhaps a manhole cover, portraying one of several possible pagan gods. The most famous characteristic of the Mouth, however, is its role as a lie detector. Starting from the Middle Ages, it was believed that if one told a lie with his hand in the mouth of the sculpture, it would be bitten off.
Google can be considered a contemporary lie detector, an oracle and, as Clive Thompson wrote on Wired Magazine, a "reputation-management system".
The new inter-face was pretty successful in the first user-testing session: nobody out of seven connected it to the "Mouth of Truth", but everybody saw the human face and that discovery was rewarding and motivational for continuing the experience.

21 November 2006
Eliza and the Eliza Effect
The limitations of the word-by-word Google Talk approach became quickly clear: not only the mechanism was way too slow to engage people, but also the sentences generated were often disjointed and rarely made sense. Although the Google's sibylline discourse had some fascination, "Search engine literature" was not one of my aims, so I started looking for an interaction model that was closer to a Web chat.
One of the first and most famous experiments in artificial intelligent is Eliza, a 1966 computer program designed by Joseph Weizenbaum to simulate a psychoanalyst. You can try it here.
Eliza worked by simple parsing and substitution of key words into canned phrases. Depending upon the initial entries by the user the illusion of a human writer could be instantly dispelled, or could continue through several interchanges. Upon observation, researchers discovered users subconsciously assuming Eliza's questions implied interest in the topics discussed, even when they consciously knew that Eliza did not simulate emotion. This cognitive dissonance between the user's awareness of programming limitations and their behavior towards the output of the program became known as the "Eliza Effect".
While Weizenbaum focused on how Eliza "could induce powerful delusional thinking in quite normal people" and wrote a book (Computer Power and Human Reason, 1976) to explain that the internals of computers are not magical, Jane Murray (Hamlet on the Holodeck, 1996) analyzed the Eliza Effect in terms of drama. She viewed Eliza/Doctor as a dramatic character, Weizenbaum as a kind of playwright, and the source of the Eliza Effect in such cases as "the human propensity to suspend disbelief in the presence of a persuasive dramatic presence". Murray made visible what was not clear from Weizenbaum's and other analyses: that people enjoy Eliza. Initial interactions are often quite fun and engaging, and create the sense of open-ended possibilities with an interesting character.
There is an initial "boom" of expectations, shortly followed by a "bust", or delusion, that I obviously wished to avoid.
I created a Google's version of Eliza, based on the JavaScript code developed by Michal Wallace and George Dunlop. It was clear that this approach was not enough to keep users engaged for more than a couple of minutes, nor it was suited to get people repeating the experience after their first time. I needed a vast and open content-base (what is more open and richer than the Web?) and a more "intelligent" computational system.

29 November 2006
Alice
The search for an "intelligent" computational system was pretty quick: all links pointed to the A.L.I.C.E. project. Started by dr. Richard Wallace in 1995, it is an award-winning natural language processing chatterbot, i.e. a program that engages in a conversation with a human by applying some heuristical pattern matching rules to the human's input. Despite A.L.I.C.E. inability to pass the Turing Test (the holy Grail of artificial intelligence) it presented several benefits for my project that were decisive for choosing it against more complex natural language interpreters:

1) A.L.I.C.E. is an open-source project: a variety of clients have been developed by an enthusiastic community and are free to use and easy to implement with new functionalities

2) the program uses an XML Schema called AIML (Artificial Intelligence Markup Language) for specifying the heuristic conversation rules; XML is compatible with every programming language

3) several AIML standard sets are available for quick customization

4) A.L.I.C.E. clients produce plain text responses that can be directly passed to search engines as query strings.
My idea was to use a customized A.L.I.C.E. as an artificial intelligence filter between users and the search engine.

17 December 2006
ChatClouds
Chatting with Google is like chatting with the Web after Google.
If Google were considered a fictional character, it would embody the mainstream thoughts, opinions, tastes, concerns and interests of Web users. A profile of Google's "personality" could be drawn by tracking its conversations and mapping its most frequent topics. My aim was not to create a small-scale replica of Google Zeitgeist, but to generate two different profiles:

1) the average user, gathering data from all users conversations (my little Zeitgeist)

2) the average mediated user, gathering data from all Google's responses.
If Google were neutral, the two profiles should virtually match, proving that the biggest search engine is accurately reflecting the global Zeitgeist. But if the difference between the two profiles were significant, then it would be clear that Google is influencing or even manipulating millions of people's clickstreams. This assumption is not rocket science but rather a personal interpretation of data-mining evidence: "questioning the transparency of Google" was later amended from the project's scope.
The tool chosen to visualize the two aggregated profiles is commonly known as tagcloud. A tag is a descriptive word attached to a document (usually a webpage), and a tagcloud is a weighted list of tags, where the most popular are depicted in a larger font or otherwise emphasized. An inspirational work was US Presidential Speeches Tag Cloud by Chirag Mehta, that shows the popularity, frequency, and trends in the usages of words within speeches, official documents, declarations, and letters written by the Presidents of the USA between 1776 and 2007.
I began experimenting with tagclouds and developed a small independent project, labeled This is not, where tags are used as negative definitions: given a picture, users are prompted to write what that picture is not.

13 January 2007
The mRank
Being the chatterbot's role clear, I needed a rule (see some experiments) for filtering search results, which would then be translated into a ranking algorithm.
The one I developed takes into account both the number of occurrences of A.L.I.C.E.'s single words (WordsRank) in Google's results snippets (the short texts describing each link), and the position of the snippet within the result's page (PageRank) that indicates its relevancy. Being WordsRank a quantitative number (the higher, the better) while PageRank is a qualitative number (the lower, the better), a unique ranking value can be obtained by subtracting PageRank from WordsRank.
GlobalRank = WordsRank - PageRank.
My application's basic flow is therefore:

1) user types and submits a text

2) A.L.I.C.E. interpreter generates a response

3) A.L.I.C.E.'s response text is searched in Google

4) Google Search results are processed in order to extract the sentence with the highest GlobalRank

5) the extracted sentence is displayed to the user.

20 February 2007
GoogleSpeaks video
Questioning the transparency of Google was still my "mission" when I produced a short video for the MA mid-term show, which took place at the Westbourne Studios in West London, from February 20 to February 22, 2007.
Some notes about it:
Google is not transparent, both in terms of content and of expression, because it deeply influences how and what we experience on the Web. It is implicit that Google wants/tries to be transparent in front of its users in many ways:

.simple interface (no distractions)

.organic results separated from sponsored ones

.sponsored results are always contextual

.fast results

.results based on computational ranking, no human editing (generally)
Google’s aim is to become completely transparent, where the perfect user experience would be:

1) user inputs keyword(s)

2) Google provides one result only, and redirects to such result webpage, which is the “I’m Feeling Lucky” mode extended to all searches. As stated in their corporate philosophy page "Google may be the only company in the world whose stated goal is to have users leave its website as quickly as possible: the less time people spend searching, the better for Google (it means they found what they wanted, so their inputs are more relevant).
Google relies on web democracy because its ranking comes from millions of users’ “votes” (links) but its position is that of an organizer. Google organizes the Web, regardless how this is done, and this is a position of power and control: technocracy is the keyword.
The Web is enormous, we (users) seek simplicity, Google gives us a simple gateway to the Web. Here comes the restriction. Willing or not, we have to pass through Google’s input box to get where we want.

I decided to abandon any "Identity Correction" scope after an eye-opening argument that followed a presentation given to first and second years Digital Media students. Furthermore, I wanted to avoid the "So what?" effect that in my opinion characterizes a considerable amount of contemporary art works. Instead of criticizing, I could exploit Google's services to build an useful and entertaining tool. It may be naive, but this new perspective was immediately motivational to me.

13 March 2007
Filtering options
Given the huge amount of pornographic or otherwise offending material that can be found on the Web, “Safe Search” has become a standard solution offered by any search engine to give users different options to filter off undesirable results. Google, as many others, has three filtering settings. I decided to exploit this convention to offer users three modes of interaction with my application:
  • Strict filtering: users interact directly with the chatterbot, so that the responses they are given come from an internal database. This mode is useful to get familiar with the behaviours of the artificial intelligence software, and in a further stage (not yet developed as it requires user’s subscription) it will allow people to teach the chatterbot how they wish it to answer, developing their personalized set of patterns. It can be considered a learning mode.
  • Moderate filtering: in this case users inputs are passed to the chatterbot, its response searched in Google and then one sentence extracted from Google’s results. This is the default setting and it allows bringing Web content into the discourse while maintaining the appearance of a conversation.
  • No filtering: this mode turns off artificial intelligence, so that users are directly confronted with Web content. The behaviour of this setting can be considered similar to that of Google's I’m Feeling Lucky, where the application displays a searching history rather than a proper conversation.

    09 April 2007
    Why NooSpeak?
    The name "Google" is a playful misspelling of Googol, a mathematical nickname for the number made by the digit 1 followed by hundred zeros (1.0e100). NooSpeak's reference instead is the fictional language used by party members in George Orwell's "1984": NewSpeak. Orwell included an essay about it in the form of an Appendix, in which the basic principles of the language are explained. Newspeak is closely based on English but has a greatly reduced and simplified vocabulary and grammar. This suited the totalitarian regime of the Party, whose aim was to make any alternative thinking ("thoughtcrime") or speech impossible by removing any words or possible constructs which describe the ideas of freedom, rebellion and so on. Yet Orwell didn't consider that NewSpeak strict logic and simplified grammar would actually fit artificial intelligence needs: for example if "better" became "gooder" and "best" became "goodest" the computational process of interpreting and generating sentences would be simpler.
    In my idea, NooSpeak is evoking a new language and a new way of interaction between humans and artificial intelligence systems, as well as underlining the dialogical approach to Web search. The logotype design was an organic process, where the only guideline was that of communicating the union of search and chat experiences in one application. The chosen logotype plays on duality both with colors and icons, with the double "o" representing the standard icons for search (a magnifier) and chat (a comic balloon).

    Authorized Public Interface
    The coding method I used to extract chatting responses from Google’s results pages proved to be inefficient, as it relied on HTML scraping techniques, so that every time Google changed their formats (it happened three times within six months) I had to adapt my PHP string parser. The solution was to use the AJAX Search API that Google provides upon subscription and allows to hook any application with their search engine database via JavaScript. As a result, I also decided to ask an official authorization for using Google’s name and brand features. I sent this image to test their flexibility with formal elements, and as expected Google’s reply pointed out that “…the mock-up that you sent very closely resembles the Google homepage. We think that the current layout in combination with the use of our name could confuse users.” I therefore sent an updated layout of the homepage and obtained the authorization.

    Hyperlinks
    The power of the Web relies on its hypertextual nature, which from a user’s perspective is the potential of making any resource just one click away. Google’s PageRank also relies on hyperlinks, which are interpreted as votes: the more a Web resource is hyperlinked, the more valuable is considered. Put in a simple syllogism: the Web would simply not exist without hyperlinks, my application aims to be a “chat with the Web”, therefore it needs hyperlinks.
    Using the Google Search API made their implementation an easy step, as results come nicely divided in their components: title, snippet (description), url, cached url and so on. Every NooSpeak's entry (in moderate and unfiltered modes) is therefore a link to the Web resource from which Noospeak's sentence was extracted.

    Frameworks for the future
    NooSpeak is at the moment a growing patchwork of different applications: a chatterbot, a search engine interface, a ranking software and a tagcloud generator. There is a clear need for restructuring the code architecture and for standardization of the various components, on a solid platform that also allows for future extensions or modifications.
    Standardization becomes crucial when working in teams, and I am now working with two PHP developers, Daniel Infante and Walter Franchetti, to make NooSpeak a “real-life” service. We decided to use the Zend framework, which is an open-source project supported by a large community of developers and major companies (such as IBM), with the aim of providing an architecture for developing entire applications with no other library dependencies. It is very comprehensive, well documented, and relatively easy to use.
